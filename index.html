Project Overview

Abstract & Introduction

Wildfires in West Africa's moist tropical forests are increasing as drought, forest fragmentation, and climate change render these ecosystems progressively more susceptible to burning. A substantial share of events are low-severity surface fires advancing beneath closed canopy, which standard global burned area products routinely fail to detect.

This case study, sponsored by Dr. Michael Wimberly at the Data Institute for Societal Challenges (DISC), addresses that detection gap across the East-West Guinean Lowland Forests ecoregion using VIIRS active fire hotspot detections validated against Landsat imagery through Google Earth Engine.

This work contributes a methodological framework to better identify "hidden" forest fires by transforming raw thermal heat points into approximate burned area maps - addressing the canopy gap that leaves forest managers unaware of ongoing encroachment. - Kesani & Pandem, 2026

The methodological core transforms discrete point detections into spatially continuous burned area estimates through a spatiotemporal clustering and interpolation pipeline, yielding gridded burn date and fire radiative power surfaces at 375 m resolution across a 12-year study period.

Analytical Pipeline

Methodology

A five-stage pipeline transforms raw thermal anomalies into validated burned area estimates and fire behavior metrics across the 12-year archive.

1
Data Ingestion

VIIRS SNPP Active Fire Archive (2013-2024)

Annual CSV files were downloaded from NASA FIRMS for all African countries. Records were filtered to the East-West Guinean Lowland Forests ecoregion boundary and reprojected to Albers Equal Area Africa (ESRI:102022) for metric calculations. Duplicate geometries were removed to prevent spurious graph edges.

375 m I-band resolution Suomi NPP satellite Nominal + High confidence Type 0 (vegetation fires)
2
Spatiotemporal Clustering

Delaunay Triangulation + Graph-Based Event Assignment

A Delaunay triangulation was constructed over all fire points, producing a sparse candidate neighbor set. For every candidate edge, the pipeline computes planar distance and absolute day-of-year difference. Edges are retained only if they satisfy both spatial (D < Dmax) and temporal (|DOY| ≤ Tmax) thresholds. Connected components via igraph define discrete fire events.

Fig. 4 - Spatiotemporal Clustering Pipeline
Dmax = 2000 m (optimal) Tmax = 2 days (optimal) igraph connected components tri2nb / dnearneigh
3
Polygon Construction

Concave Hull Burned Area Footprints

For each cluster with n ≥ 3 member detections, the concaveman algorithm (concavity c = 2) generates a precise boundary that conforms to the irregular geometry of the point cloud, indenting around unburned forest gaps rather than bridging them like standard convex hulls. All annual hulls are merged into a single event-polygon layer.

Fig. 5 - Convex vs. Concave Hull Comparison
4
Interpolation

Nearest-Neighbor and IDW Gap-Filling at 375 m

Inside each event polygon, gstat-based nearest-neighbor interpolation fills burn date (DOY) for cells with no direct detections. IDW was evaluated for fire radiative power (FRP) surfaces. Observed detections take precedence over interpolated values where both are present. The result is a pair of continuous 375 m rasters per year.

gstat nearest-neighbor IDW for FRP 375 m blank raster grid 12 annual raster stacks
5
Validation

dNBR Masking + Point-Based Accuracy Assessment

Six validation sites within Ghana's forest reserves (years 2016, 2020, 2022) were evaluated using GEE-derived Landsat dNBR masks (threshold > 0.1). A stratified random sample of 1,500 points (200-point audit per site, 1:1 burned/unburned) provided Kappa, Precision, Recall, F1, Omission, and Commission metrics. Area-based geometric comparison against dNBR reference masks justified the optimal D/T configuration.

01

STL Decomposition Reveals 2015-2016 El Nino Pulse

Seasonal-Trend Decomposition via Loess (STL) applied to the monthly fire count time series confirmed a consistent rhythmic annual pulse with a pronounced surge during 2015-2016, corresponding to the El Nino-induced drought.

Fire activity is heavily concentrated within a narrow burn window spanning March through April - the period of maximum forest vulnerability. More recent years (2021-2024) exhibit a broader and flatter seasonal distribution, suggesting a lengthening fire season consistent with increasing drought stress.

02

North-to-South Latitudinal Fire Wave

Spatial mapping indicates fire activity is organized along a quasi-stationary frontier at the forest-savanna transition zone. A detailed seasonal analysis of the 2016 archive reveals a distinct north-to-south wave of fire progression.

Fires initiate at higher latitudes (7.0-7.5 N) early in the calendar year and migrate progressively southward toward the forest interior as the dry season intensifies. High-intensity detections (> 7.8 MW) are concentrated along the northern margin and eastern corridor.

Low-SD pixels in the western forest block burn at nearly the same time annually, suggesting structurally entrenched recurring fire exposure rather than opportunistic ignitions.

03

Optimal Parameters: D = 2000 m, T = 2 Days

Distance thresholds of 500-2500 m and temporal windows of 1-3 days were tested across both the Nominal-High (NH) and Nominal-High-Low (NHL) confidence subsets. The model reaches a balanced spatial configuration at D = 2000 m.

Although NHL produces marginally higher mean Kappa (0.590 vs 0.588), the difference is negligible relative to inter-site variability (SD ~0.12). Low-confidence detections introduce commission error from non-forest heat sources without meaningful accuracy gain. NH maintains higher precision (0.856 vs 0.846).

05

Tinte Bepo and Opro River - Upward Fire Trends

The non-parametric Mann-Kendall test distinguishes significant monotonic trends (P < 0.05) from stochastic noise across 12 Ghana forest reserves. The 2016 El Nino spike is universally visible but correctly isolated from long-term trends.

Tano Ofin (P = 1) correctly shows no systematic change despite an anomalous 20,000 Ha 2016 spike. Tain II presents a paradox: significant downward trend (P = 0.016) despite highest raw burned area, likely indicating biomass exhaustion rather than recovery.

Summary

Conclusion

This case study demonstrates a spatiotemporal pipeline for the East-West Guinean Lowland Forests that extends beyond static pixel counts toward characterizing the spatial extent and seasonal behavior of forest fire encroachment. By documenting 1.03 million hectares of burned area - a 14.1% gain over the MODIS global standard - the approach provides a refined monitoring baseline for fires that may be obscured by the forest canopy.

The Mann-Kendall trend analysis identifies Tinte Bepo and Opro River as reserves with statistically significant upward fire trajectories, while the STL decomposition captures the 2015-2016 El Nino-induced fire pulse that dominated the archive's most active period. These findings illustrate the practical value of this framework as a complement to existing global fire monitoring products.

Key Contributions

A spatiotemporal clustering approach using Delaunay triangulation and igraph connected components - extending individual pixel-based detection methods toward event-level fire mapping.

Concave hull polygon generation using concaveman (c=2) providing ecologically grounded burned area footprints that conform to irregular forest incursion geometry.

Parameter sensitivity analysis across a 5x3 distance-time threshold matrix validated against six Landsat dNBR reference sites in Ghana, supporting informed threshold selection.

12-year fire behavior characterization including season onset, length, interannual timing consistency, and peak FRP across the ecoregion at 375 m resolution.
